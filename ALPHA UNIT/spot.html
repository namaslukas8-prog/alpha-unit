Example 3: Fake PayPal Payment Failure

From: paypa1-secure@paypalservice.net

Subject: Your PayPal Payment Failed - Action Required

Your recent payment could not be processed due to security issues. Update your payment method immediately to avoid service interruption and account suspension.

UPDATE PAYMENT INFORMATION

üö© Show red flags
  • Domain Typo: "paypa1" (with number 1) instead of "paypal"
  • Wrong Domain Extension: Real PayPal uses @paypal.com, not @paypalservice.net
  • Unusual Request: PayPal never asks to update payment via email links
  • Vague Language: No specific transaction ID, amount, or date mentioned
  • Threat of Suspension: Creates fear to bypass logic
  • Professional Appearance: Scammers copy the real PayPal design and tone

What to do: Log into paypal.com directly (don't click the link). Check your account history. Call PayPal support if there's a real issue.

Example 4: Bank Account Verification Email

From: security@barclaysbank-update.co.uk

Subject: Barclays: Verify Your Identity

Dear Barclays Customer,

Due to recent security enhancements, we need to verify your banking details. Please confirm your full name, account number, sort code, and PIN code by replying to this email or clicking below:

VERIFY DETAILS

üö© Show red flags
  • Wrong Domain: Real Barclays uses barclays.com, not barclaysbank-update.co.uk
  • Never Asks for PIN: Real banks NEVER ask for PIN or full account details via email
  • Red Alert Request: Banks never verify sensitive data through email or web forms
  • Generic Greeting: "Dear Barclays Customer" not personalized
  • Suspicious Reasoning: "Security enhancements" as excuse to collect data
  • Email Reply Risk: Asking to reply to email puts sensitive info in email system

What to do: Hang up and call your bank directly using the number on your bank card or official website. Real banks verify you through secure login portals, not email.

Pop-up Message (appears on screen):

  • Unexpected Pop-up: Windows doesn't warn you with urgent pop-ups like this
  • Threatening Language: "47 viruses", "at risk" designed to panic you

  • "Don't Close": This is a red flag ‚Äì legitimate warnings never use this language

üö© Show red flags
  • Unrealistic Earnings: $5,000/week for 10 hours is $500/hour ‚Äì not realistic for data entry

  • Suspicious Promise: "Refunded after first week" rarely happens

From: Profile "Michael" on dating app (after 2 weeks of chatting)

üö© Show red flags
  • Quick Emotional Connection: They profess love/commitment very quickly
  • Avoiding Video Chat: If asked, they always have excuses (bad camera, no internet)
  • Sudden Emergency: After building trust, they request money for an "emergency"
  • Money Request Escalation: Starts small, increases over time
  • Overseas Story: Common excuse for not meeting in person
  • Wire Transfer Urgency: "Wire money quickly" ‚Äì untraceable method
  • Promise to Repay: They'll always promise to pay back (they won't)
  • Generic Photos: Profile photos are often stolen from real people

What to do: Hang up immediately. Reverse image search their photos. Real romantic interests will meet in person and won't ask for money. Report the profile to the dating app.

Key Takeaways

  • Verify through official channels: When in doubt, contact the company directly using the number on their official website or your physical card
  • Hover over links: Before clicking, hover your mouse to see the real URL (not the display text)
  • Check the sender: Look closely at email addresses ‚Äì typos like "paypa1" or wrong domains are red flags
  • Legitimate companies never: Ask for passwords, PINs, or full account details via email; request payment through email links; threaten immediate account closure
  • Trust your instincts: If something feels off or too good to be true, it probably is
  • When in doubt, say no: It's better to be cautious than to fall victim to a scam
  • Report scams: Report phishing to the real company and to the Anti-Phishing Working Group (apwg.org)
‚úì Always verify by contacting the company directly through their official website or phone number, never by clicking email links. When in doubt, ask someone you trust!
