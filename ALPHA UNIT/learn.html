Phishing is when attackers trick you into clicking a link, opening an attachment, or sharing personal information by impersonating legitimate organizations.

Common Phishing Signs:

  • Sender address doesn't match the organization's real domain
  • Urgency: "Act now!", "Verify immediately", "Account suspended"
  • Requests for passwords, credit cards, or personal information
  • Generic greetings: "Dear Customer" instead of your name
  • Suspicious links or attachments
  • Spelling and grammar errors
  • Too good to be true offers or threats

How to Stay Safe:

  • Never click links in unexpected emails—go directly to the official website
  • Hover over links to preview the actual destination
  • Check the sender's email address carefully (attackers use similar domains: "paypa1.com" instead of "paypal.com")
  • Enable two-factor authentication on important accounts
  • Use email filters and spam detection tools
  • Report suspicious emails to your organization or the real company
  • Be cautious with file attachments—even from known contacts
  • Verify requests through another communication channel (call the company directly)

What to Do if You Fall for Phishing:

  • Change your password immediately if you entered it
  • Enable two-factor authentication
  • Monitor your account for suspicious activity
  • Report it to the real organization and your email provider
  • Consider identity theft monitoring services

Your password is often the only thing standing between your account and an attacker. Weak passwords are one of the most common entry points for hackers.

What Makes a Strong Password:

  • At least 12–16 characters long (longer is better)
  • Mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols
  • No personal information (birthdate, pet name, address)
  • No dictionary words or common patterns (password123)
  • Unique for each account
  • Not reused across multiple websites

Why Reusing Passwords is Dangerous:

  • If one website is breached, attackers try that password everywhere
  • Criminals use leaked password lists for "credential stuffing" attacks
  • One weak site can compromise all your accounts

Password Manager Benefits:

  • Securely stores all your passwords in encrypted vaults
  • Generates random, strong passwords automatically
  • Fills passwords automatically (saves time and reduces typos)
  • Protects against phishing by only filling passwords on legitimate sites
  • Syncs across devices

Recommended Password Managers:

  • Bitwarden (open-source, affordable)
  • 1Password (user-friendly, strong security)
  • KeePass (offline, fully private)
  • LastPass (popular, many integrations)

Password Best Practices:

  • Create a unique, strong password for each important account
  • Use passphrases (4+ random words) for better memorability
  • Never share passwords via email or text
  • Don't save passwords in browsers (unless using a password manager)
  • Change passwords if you suspect a breach
  • Use a password manager to track all your accounts

Multi-factor authentication adds a second (or more) verification step beyond your password. Even if someone has your password, they can't access your account without the second factor.

Types of MFA:

  • Authenticator Apps (Best): Generate time-based codes—Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, Authy
  • SMS Text Messages: Codes sent via text (convenient but less secure)
  • Email Codes: Similar to SMS but sent via email
  • Biometric (Fingerprint/Face): Some apps support facial recognition or fingerprint
  • Hardware Keys (Most Secure): USB devices like YubiKey—impossible to phish
  • Push Notifications: Approve login from another trusted device

Why Authenticator Apps are Better Than SMS:

  • SMS can be intercepted or rerouted (SIM swapping attacks)
  • Authenticator apps generate codes offline—can't be intercepted
  • SMS requires phone signal; apps work anytime

MFA Setup Steps:

  • Go to account settings in your online account
  • Find "Security" or "Two-Factor Authentication"
  • Download an authenticator app (Google Authenticator or Authy)
  • Scan the QR code in the app
  • Save backup codes in a secure location
  • Enable MFA and test it

Protect Your Backup Codes:

  • Save them in a password manager or secure location
  • Never share them via email or text
  • Store them offline if possible
  • Generate new codes if you suspect they've been compromised

Enable MFA On These Accounts First:

  • Email (most important—controls password resets)
  • Banking and financial accounts
  • Social media accounts
  • Work/professional accounts
  • Cloud storage (Google Drive, OneDrive, iCloud)

Social engineering is when attackers manipulate you psychologically to divulge information or take actions that compromise your security.

Common Social Engineering Tactics:

  • Impersonation: Pretending to be from your bank, IT support, tax agency
  • Urgency: "Your account will be closed!", "Act immediately!"
  • Authority: Claiming to represent official organizations
  • Trust Building: Building rapport before asking for sensitive info
  • Fear: Threats of account closure or legal action
  • Reward: Offering prizes, refunds, or deals

Common Scams by Type:

  • Tech Support Scams: Pop-ups claiming your device has viruses
  • Prize/Lottery Scams: "You've won!" (but didn't enter)
  • Romance Scams: Building emotional connection then asking for money
  • Loan Scams: Guaranteed loans with upfront fees
  • Job Scams: Work-from-home jobs that ask for upfront payment
  • IRS/Tax Scams: Threatening legal action over taxes
  • Package/Delivery Scams: Fake shipping notifications with malicious links

How to Protect Yourself:

  • Never give personal information to unsolicited callers (hang up and call the official number)
  • Verify requests through another channel (if "bank" calls, call them back at the number on your card)
  • Be suspicious of urgency and threats
  • Real companies never ask for passwords via email or phone
  • Legitimate organizations won't ask you to buy gift cards for payments
  • Check for spelling/grammar errors in emails
  • Use your instincts—if something feels off, it probably is
  • Take time to think before responding (scammers pressure you to act fast)

If You Fall for a Scam:

  • Stop all contact with the scammer immediately
  • Report it to the FTC (ftc.gov), FBI, or local police
  • Report it to your bank if money was transferred
  • Monitor your accounts for unauthorized activity
  • Consider placing a fraud alert with credit bureaus

Public Wi-Fi at cafes, airports, and hotels is convenient but risky. Attackers can easily intercept unencrypted data and create fake networks.

Risks of Public Wi-Fi:

  • Unencrypted traffic can be intercepted (passwords, credit cards)
  • Attackers can create fake networks ("Evil Twin" networks)
  • Man-in-the-middle attacks intercept communication
  • Malware distribution through compromised networks
  • Cookie theft (attackers hijack your login sessions)

What NOT to Do on Public Wi-Fi:

  • Don't access banking or financial accounts
  • Don't enter credit card information
  • Don't change passwords
  • Don't access sensitive work files
  • Don't make purchases on untrusted sites

How to Stay Safe on Public Wi-Fi:

  • Use a VPN (Virtual Private Network) to encrypt all traffic
  • Only visit HTTPS websites (padlock icon in browser)
  • Disable auto-connect and file sharing on your device
  • Turn off Bluetooth when not in use
  • Verify the network name with café staff (avoid unnamed networks)
  • Use mobile hotspot instead (tether from your phone)
  • Avoid accessing sensitive accounts (email, banking)
  • Use two-factor authentication on all important accounts
  • Keep your firewall enabled

Best VPN Services:

  • ExpressVPN (fast, reliable, no-log policy)
  • ProtonVPN (privacy-focused, free option available)
  • NordVPN (strong encryption, good interface)
  • Mullvad (extremely privacy-conscious)

Software updates patch security vulnerabilities that attackers actively exploit. Delaying updates leaves you vulnerable to known attacks.

What Updates Do:

  • Patch security vulnerabilities and exploits
  • Fix bugs that could lead to crashes or data loss
  • Improve performance and add new features
  • Remove outdated protocols attackers can exploit
  • Strengthen compatibility with secure services

What Needs Regular Updates:

  • Operating Systems: Windows, macOS, Linux, iOS, Android
  • Web Browsers: Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge
  • Applications: Office software, media players, PDF readers
  • Plugins: Java, Flash (if still used), media codecs
  • Firmware: Router, printers, security cameras

How to Stay Updated:

  • Enable automatic updates for your OS and browsers
  • Set updates to install during off-hours (avoid disruption)
  • Restart your devices regularly (some updates require restart)
  • Check for app updates weekly in your app store
  • Remove old software you no longer use (less to update)
  • Don't delay critical security updates
  • Uninstall outdated plugins like Flash

Important Security Updates to Prioritize:

  • Operating system patches (especially critical and important)
  • Browser updates (daily exposure to threats)
  • Java and Flash (high-value targets)
  • Antivirus/security software definitions
  • Email client updates

Malware is malicious software designed to infect your device and steal data, monitor your activity, or damage your system.

Types of Malware:

  • Viruses: Replicate and spread, often destructive
  • Worms: Self-spreading, consume bandwidth and resources
  • Trojans: Disguise as legitimate software, create backdoors
  • Ransomware: Encrypts files and demands payment
  • Spyware: Monitors your activity (keylogging, screen capture)
  • Adware: Displays unwanted ads and collects browsing data
  • Rootkits: Hide deep in your system, hard to detect

How Malware Spreads:

  • Suspicious email attachments
  • Downloads from untrusted websites
  • Infected USB drives or external media
  • Software cracks or pirated software
  • Compromised websites and ads
  • Peer-to-peer file sharing

Antivirus Best Practices:

  • Keep antivirus software updated daily
  • Run full system scans weekly or monthly
  • Enable real-time scanning
  • Use reputable antivirus providers (Windows Defender, Norton, Kaspersky)
  • Quarantine suspicious files (don't delete immediately)
  • Consider malware-specialized tools (Malwarebytes)

How to Avoid Malware:

  • Never download software from untrusted sources
  • Be cautious with email attachments
  • Don't open suspicious PDF or Office files
  • Avoid peer-to-peer file sharing and torrents
  • Don't use pirated or cracked software
  • Use ad blockers to prevent malicious ads
  • Disable auto-play for CDs and USB drives
  • Keep your firewall enabled

If You Get Malware:

  • Disconnect from the internet (for advanced threats)
  • Run a full antivirus scan in Safe Mode
  • Use recovery tools like Malwarebytes or Windows Defender Offline
  • Consider a clean OS reinstall if severely infected
  • Change all passwords from a clean computer
  • Monitor accounts for fraudulent activity
